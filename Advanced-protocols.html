Advanced Fat Loss Stack

This is BioLinked's summary of a six-compound fat-loss protocol outlined by Hunter Williams. The approach targets fat loss through multiple mechanisms simultaneously — appetite suppression, metabolic acceleration, visceral fat targeting, and cellular-level fat oxidation — rather than relying on any single pathway.

Each compound plays a distinct role, and the stack is designed so the compounds complement rather than duplicate each other. Below is a condensed overview of each, followed by a summary table and a synergy note.

⚠ Research Use Only. Several compounds in this protocol are investigational research agents not approved for general human use. This is educational reference material only — not medical advice. Any use should be under the supervision of a qualified healthcare provider.

Retatrutide

GLP-1 / GIP / Glucagon Triple Agonist
Dose1–2 mg
Frequency3× / week (M/W/F AM)
Duration12–16 weeks
RouteSubcutaneous

Why it's in the stack: Retatrutide is a next-generation GLP-1 class compound that targets three metabolic pathways at once — appetite suppression, insulin regulation, and caloric burn. Hunter cites clinical trial data showing roughly 23–24% body weight reduction at higher doses over 11 months, and striking improvements in liver fat markers. Its primary role in the stack is appetite control and metabolic reset.

Administration Notes

Typical research-grade vial is 10 mg lyophilized powder. Reconstitute with 2 mL of bacteriostatic water, yielding a 5 mg/mL concentration. On a U-100 insulin syringe, 20 units = 1 mg dose. Gentle swirl to mix (never shake), store refrigerated after mixing, and rotate injection sites.

Side Effect Profile

Mainly gastrointestinal — nausea, vomiting, diarrhea — especially in the first weeks. These usually subside as the body adapts. Contraindicated in anyone with a history of medullary thyroid carcinoma or MEN2.

MOTS-c

Mitochondrial Peptide — Exercise Mimetic
Dose1 mg
Frequency5× / week (M–F AM)
Duration12–16 weeks
RouteSubcutaneous

Why it's in the stack: MOTS-c is a mitochondrial-derived peptide sometimes called the "exercise signal" — your muscles naturally release it during workouts. It activates AMPK and shifts cells toward burning fat for fuel and improving insulin sensitivity. Hunter's framing: it mimics some of the metabolic benefits of exercise at the cellular level, which pairs well with Retatrutide's appetite-side action.

Administration Notes

Same math as Retatrutide — 10 mg vial + 2 mL BAC water = 5 mg/mL, so 20 units on a U-100 insulin syringe equals 1 mg. Inject in the morning to align with natural circadian energy patterns and avoid any sleep disruption from AMPK-driven stimulation.

Side Effect Profile

Generally mild — some users report transient increases in heart rate, occasional jitteriness, and injection site irritation. Banned in competitive sports (WADA). Formal safety data in humans is limited, so medical supervision is wise.

Tesamorelin

GHRH Analog — FDA-Approved
Dose1 mg
Frequency5× / week (M–F PM)
Duration12–16 weeks
RouteSubcutaneous, bedtime

Why it's in the stack: Tesamorelin (brand name Egrifta) stimulates your pituitary to release more growth hormone, which preferentially targets visceral fat — the deep belly fat around your organs. It's the only FDA-approved compound in this stack. Hunter uses it specifically to address stubborn abdominal fat and to help preserve lean muscle during the caloric deficit created by the other compounds.

Administration Notes

Same reconstitution as above. Night dosing is strategic — natural GH pulses are highest during sleep, so bedtime injections closely mimic physiology and may improve sleep quality. Avoid carb-heavy meals in the 1–2 hours before injection to maximize GH release.

Side Effect Profile

Most common issue is injection site reactions (up to ~25% of users). GH's fluid-retention effects can cause edema, joint stiffness, or tingling. Can mildly raise blood glucose (A1C +0.3–0.5%). Contraindicated in anyone with active cancer or cancer history due to IGF-1 elevation.

5-Amino-1MQ

NNMT Inhibitor — Cellular Metabolism
Dose1 mg (research) or capsules
Frequency5× / week (M–F AM)
Duration12–16 weeks (8-week break optional)
RouteOral or SubQ

Why it's in the stack: 5-Amino-1MQ blocks NNMT, an enzyme in fat cells that slows metabolism and encourages fat storage. Blocking it raises NAD+ levels and shifts fat cells from hoarding energy to burning it. Preclinical data showed mice losing fat mass and gaining insulin sensitivity without changes to diet or activity. Hunter's framing: it's a cellular-level metabolic enhancer that complements the hormonal action of the other compounds.

Administration Notes

Often available in both oral capsule form and research-grade powder for reconstitution. Oral has decent bioavailability. Take in the morning, ideally with breakfast to avoid mild stomach upset.

Side Effect Profile

Generally mild — light nausea, occasional fatigue or dizziness during initial adjustment. No stimulant-like effects. Long-term human safety data is still limited.

SLU-PP-332

ERR Agonist — Exercise Mimetic (Oral)
Dose250–500 µg
FrequencyDaily (morning)
Duration12–16 weeks
RouteOral capsule

Why it's in the stack: SLU-PP-332 activates ERR (Estrogen-Related Receptors), turning on the same genes that endurance training activates — essentially tricking the body into "training for a marathon" at the cellular level. In animal studies it increased resting calorie burn and endurance without appetite suppression. Hunter uses it as a non-stimulant way to raise baseline metabolism, pairing with Retatrutide (eat less) to maximize the "burn more" side.

Administration Notes

Oral only — no reconstitution needed. Take on an empty stomach in the morning for best absorption. Typically comes in capsules from research suppliers.

Side Effect Profile

No serious side effects observed in animal testing. Not a stimulant — no jitters or heart rate spikes. Possible mild digestive shifts as fuel utilization changes.

ATX-304

Pan-AMPK Activator — Energy Expenditure
Dose100–200 mg
FrequencyDaily (morning)
Duration12–16 weeks
RouteOral capsule

Why it's in the stack: ATX-304 is a pan-AMPK activator — essentially a more powerful, pharmacological version of what metformin and exercise do to cellular metabolism. AMPK is the cell's master energy sensor; activating it tells the body to burn stored fuel rather than store more. Hunter points to animal data showing near-complete reduction in body fat with treated mice eating normally. Its role in the stack is to dramatically raise energy expenditure while the other compounds handle appetite and visceral fat.

Administration Notes

Oral capsule, morning dosing. Start with 100 mg; some users move up to 200 mg after tolerance is established. Take with water, with or without food.

Side Effect Profile

Early human testing (~150 people) has been favorable. Possible transient fatigue or lightheadedness from rapid blood sugar uptake. Watch for hypoglycemia symptoms if also on diabetes meds. Long-term safety still being studied.

How they work together

The stack design is deliberate — no compound is redundant. Retatrutide handles appetite, Tesamorelin targets visceral fat and preserves muscle, MOTS-c and SLU-PP-332 mimic different aspects of exercise (AMPK vs ERR pathways), 5-Amino-1MQ optimizes fat cells at the enzyme level, and ATX-304 amplifies whole-body energy expenditure. Together they hit every major lever of weight management: eat less, burn more, store less, mobilize fat, and preserve muscle.

⚠ Important: Introduce one compound at a time when starting a stack like this — not all six on day one. That way you can identify tolerance and attribute any side effects correctly. Regular bloodwork (lipids, glucose, liver enzymes, IGF-1) before and mid-cycle is strongly advised.

Peptide Reconstitution Guide

This is BioLinked's summary of Hunter Williams' guide to peptide reconstitution — the process of turning freeze-dried peptide powder into a usable injectable solution. Hunter's central argument: learning this skill yourself removes one of the biggest barriers to using peptides effectively, and the math is much simpler than it first appears.

💡 Hunter's core point: The biggest difference between people who get results with peptides and people who don't is simply learning how to reconstitute and then actually doing it. It's forgiving, the needles are tiny, and the math reduces to basic ratios once you see it.
Step 1

Gather Your Supplies

Set up a clean workspace and have everything laid out before you start. You'll need:

Peptide Vial

Small glass vial with freeze-dried powder. Check the label for the peptide name and total amount (e.g., 5 mg Tesamorelin, 10 mg BPC-157).

Bacteriostatic Water (BAC Water)

Sterile water with 0.9% benzyl alcohol added to prevent bacterial growth. This lets you use the reconstituted vial multiple times over weeks if refrigerated.

Mixing / Withdrawing Syringe

Larger 3 mL or 5 mL syringe with a 21–23 gauge needle. Used to measure BAC water and transfer it into the peptide vial.

Insulin Syringes (U-100)

Small 0.3 mL, 0.5 mL, or 1 mL syringes with fine short needles. Marked in "Units" where 100 units = 1 mL. These are what you'll use for the actual injection.

Alcohol Swabs & Sharps Container

Individually wrapped sterile swabs to clean vial tops and injection sites. A puncture-proof sharps container is non-negotiable for safe needle disposal.

Step 2

Choose Your Water Volume

Hunter's guideline for how much BAC water to add — not strict rules, just starting points that keep your injection volumes in an easy-to-measure range (usually 10–40 units on a U-100 insulin syringe):

Vial Contains Add This Much BAC Water
1–5 mg1 mL
6–10 mg2 mL
More than 10 mg3 mL
🥤 The Kool-Aid analogy: Think of the peptide powder as Kool-Aid powder and BAC water as the water you mix it with. Adding more or less water doesn't change how much Kool-Aid powder you started with — it just changes how strong each sip is. Same with peptides: the amount of water you add doesn't change the total peptide in the vial, only the concentration.
Step 3

The Mixing Process

  1. Prep the vials Pop off the plastic caps. Wipe both rubber stoppers with alcohol swabs. Let them air dry.
  2. Draw the BAC water Use your larger mixing syringe. Pull back the plunger to draw in air equal to your water volume. Stick the needle through the BAC water vial stopper, flip upside down, push the air in, then pull back the plunger to draw your water. Tap out any bubbles.
  3. Add water to the peptide Insert the needle through the peptide vial stopper at an angle. Stream the water down the glass wall — don't blast it directly onto the powder. Some peptides are delicate.
  4. Mix gently — never shake Remove the needle. Gently swirl the vial in circles or roll it between your hands until the powder fully dissolves and the liquid is clear. Shaking can damage some peptides.
  5. Label and refrigerate Write the reconstitution date on the vial. Store in the refrigerator between 2–8°C (36–46°F). Do not freeze unless specifically instructed.
Step 4

Calculate Your Dose

Hunter's most-repeated point about dosing: the units on your insulin syringe are NOT your dose. Units measure volume (liquid). Your dose is an amount (milligrams or micrograms). Your job is to figure out how many units of volume give you the right amount of peptide.

The Units

  • mg / mcg — weight/amount of peptide (1 mg = 1000 mcg). This is your dose.
  • mL — volume of liquid. Used when adding BAC water.
  • Units (IU) on a U-100 insulin syringe — volume marking. 100 units = 1 mL, so 10 units = 0.1 mL.

The Formula

Desired Dose ÷ Total Peptide in Vial = Volume to Inject ÷ Total BAC Water Added

Solve for "Volume to Inject" and you have how many units to draw. Keep units consistent on both sides (mcg for amounts, units for volumes works cleanly).

Example 1 — Tesamorelin
  • Vial: 10 mg Tesamorelin (10,000 mcg)
  • Water added: 2 mL (200 units)
  • Desired dose: 1 mg (1,000 mcg)

1,000 ÷ 10,000 = X ÷ 200 → X = 20

Draw 20 units on your insulin syringe = 1 mg dose
Example 2 — BPC-157
  • Vial: 5 mg BPC-157 (5,000 mcg)
  • Water added: 1 mL (100 units)
  • Desired dose: 250 mcg

250 ÷ 5,000 = X ÷ 100 → X = 5

Draw 5 units on your insulin syringe = 250 mcg dose
💡 Using peptide calculators: Online calculators can double-check your math, but understand the logic first. If you use one, make sure it's set to U-100 syringes and double-check your inputs.
Step 5

Subcutaneous Injection

Most peptides are injected subcutaneously — under the skin into the fatty layer. With fine insulin needles, it's typically painless.

Common Injection Sites

  • Abdomen — at least an inch away from the belly button. Easiest for self-injection.
  • Front or outer thigh — aim for the fatty area.
  • Upper outer glute — harder to reach solo.

Rotate sites each time to avoid irritation or lumps.

  1. Clean the site Wash hands. Swab the injection site with alcohol. Let it air dry (don't blow on it).
  2. Draw your dose Pull a fresh insulin syringe. Draw in a small amount of air equal to your dose, inject it into the peptide vial, flip upside down, and pull back just past your target volume (e.g., draw 11 or 12 units if you want 10).
  3. Clear air bubbles Needle pointing up, tap the syringe so bubbles rise. Push the plunger carefully until it reaches exactly your dose line.
  4. Inject Pinch up a fold of skin. Insert the needle fully (90° or 45° — 90° works with short insulin needles). Release the pinch. Slowly push the plunger all the way down. Wait a second. Pull straight out.
  5. Aftercare Light pressure with a cotton ball or tissue if there's any blood — don't rub. Used syringe straight into the sharps container. Never reuse needles.
Key Takeaways

Final Reminders

Be safe and clean

Sterile technique every time. Wipe stoppers, clean injection sites, fresh needles.

Know the distinction

Dose is mg/mcg (amount of peptide). Volume is mL or units (amount of liquid). They're not the same thing.

Water volume is flexible

Hunter's 1/2/3 mL guidelines are starting points, not rules. What matters is calculating the correct injection volume for whatever concentration you end up with.

Mindset matters

Approach it confidently, not fearfully. Done sensibly, peptides carry relatively low risk. Practice makes the process second nature quickly.
