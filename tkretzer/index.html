Tip 03 — Vitamin D3 + K2 · The #1 Fix
Severe Deficiency = Aggressive Replacement

Your Vit D at 11.4 is severe deficiency territory. Optimal is 50–80. The standard "take 1,000 IU/day" recommendation is for maintenance — not for correcting a deficiency this deep. Aggressive replacement protocol:

  • Vitamin D3: 10,000 IU per day for the first 12 weeks (loading dose, aligned with the peptide cycle and end-of-cycle bloodwork). After that, drop to 5,000 IU/day for maintenance once your retest shows you're in the 50–80 range.
  • Vitamin K2 (MK-7): 100–200 mcg per day, taken with the D3. Critical pairing — K2 directs the calcium that Vit D mobilizes into your bones instead of your arteries. Without K2, high-dose D3 can push calcium into soft tissue. For you specifically (your calcium is already on the high side at 10.7), K2 is non-negotiable.
  • Take with a fat-containing meal — D3 and K2 are fat-soluble; without dietary fat the absorption is poor.

What to buy: any reputable brand of "Vitamin D3 + K2 (MK-7)" combo. Costco, Amazon, Whole Foods all carry it. Look for D3 5,000 IU + K2 100 mcg per pill — take 2/day for the first 12 weeks.

What to expect: energy lift typically shows up by Week 3–4 of consistent dosing. Mood and sleep depth often improve in parallel. By Week 8 retest, you should be in the 40–60 range. If you're still under 30 at retest, we go higher; if you're at 80+, you back off to 2,000 IU/day maintenance.

Tip 04 — Sleep Apnea Screening · Strongly Recommended
"Sleeps 8–10 Hours and Still Tired" + High Hgb/Hct = Get the Test

The combination of "sleeps 8–10 hours but tired" + "Hemoglobin 18.0 and Hematocrit 52.5%" is the textbook fingerprint of obstructive sleep apnea (OSA). The body responds to nighttime oxygen drops by producing more red blood cells — that's exactly what we're seeing in your labs. Sleep apnea is the most likely root cause of your fatigue.

Two screening paths — pick one:

  • In-home sleep test (preferred for quick action): companies like Lofta, WatchPAT, Empower Sleep, or your primary care doctor's referral to a CMS-approved home sleep test provider. Cost ~$150–300, results in ~1–2 weeks. This is the fastest path.
  • Sleep specialist referral: ask your PCP for a referral to a sleep medicine physician. Insurance usually covers it. Slower (4–8 wks for appointment) but covers the full evaluation including an in-lab study if needed.

What treatment looks like if positive: CPAP is the gold standard and works for ~90% of patients. Sounds intimidating; modern machines are quiet and unobtrusive. If you have OSA and treat it, the fatigue can lift dramatically within 2–4 weeks of consistent CPAP use. Hgb/Hct also normalize over 3–6 months as the body stops over-producing red cells.

Why this matters more than the peptides: if you have OSA and don't treat it, the peptide stack still helps (mitochondrial repair is still good), but you're rowing upstream every single night. Treating the apnea + running the peptides = the curve gets steep fast.

Tip 05 — Hydration · Drink More Water
Target 90–100 oz/day · Addresses Mild Dehydration Markers

Your labs show subtle dehydration signals: slightly elevated calcium (10.7), low-normal sodium (137), BUN/Cr ratio at 14 (upper-normal end), and concentrated albumin (5.0 — upper end). None alone is alarming, but together they hint at "could drink more water."

The target: 90–100 oz/day for someone your size. That's roughly 6–7 of the standard 16 oz water bottles spread across the day. Hit this every day, not just when you remember.

Practical setup: get a 32–40 oz water bottle. Fill it twice a day minimum. Once before lunch, once after. That puts you at 64–80 oz. Top off with coffee, tea, or one more glass before bed to hit 90+. Avoid relying on thirst — by the time you feel thirsty you're already 1–2% dehydrated.

Why this matters for energy: even mild dehydration (1–2% body water loss) reduces cognitive performance, mood, and ATP production. For someone already battling fatigue, hydration is one of the highest-leverage zero-cost interventions.

Tip 07 — First-Time Injector Guide
SubQ Injection 101 · You've Got This

Every shot in this protocol is subcutaneous (SubQ) into the abdomen — just under the skin, not into muscle. The needle is short (29–31G), thinner than most eyelashes. Most people describe it as a barely-noticeable pinch.

The technique:

  • Wipe the vial top with an alcohol pad. Wait 10 seconds.
  • Pull air into the syringe equal to your dose (10 units).
  • Push the air into the vial (prevents vacuum).
  • Invert the vial and draw your 10 units.
  • Wipe the abdomen injection spot with alcohol.
  • Pinch a fold of skin (about 1 inch wide). Insert the needle at 45–90°. Push the plunger slowly.
  • Withdraw the needle. Dispose in the sharps container.

Day 1 plan: start with the MOTS-C shot (10u, fasted, upper-right abdomen). That's it for Day 1 — one compound, one shot, learning the routine. Day 2 add the SS-31 (10u, upper-left abdomen). By end of Week 1 you're running the full routine and it takes ~3 minutes total.

Mild redness or a tiny bruise at the injection site is normal and resolves within a day. If a site stays red/hot/swollen past 24 hrs, message me.
