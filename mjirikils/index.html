Tip 01 — First-Time Injector? Read This First
SubQ Injection 101 · You've Got This

You flagged never having injected before. That's completely normal — SubQ (subcutaneous) injections with insulin syringes are the easiest type of injection there is. 29–31G needles are thin — you'll barely feel them. Here's the routine:

  • Wash hands. Pull out a fresh syringe + alcohol pad.
  • Wipe the vial top with alcohol. Let it dry 5 sec.
  • Draw your dose to 10 units (the mark on the syringe).
  • Pick a spot on your abdomen, ~2″ from belly button. Wipe with alcohol.
  • Pinch a small fold of skin. Insert needle at 45–90° (perpendicular is fine for SubQ).
  • Push the plunger slowly (~5 sec). Pull needle out. Light pressure with the pad if any bleeding.
  • Drop syringe in sharps container. Done. ~60 seconds total.

Fear-management tips: (1) ice the spot 30 sec before the shot — numbs the surface, makes the needle invisible. (2) The first shot is the hardest. By shot #3 you'll wonder why you were worried. (3) The needle is shorter than most eyelashes — truly tiny.

Standard SubQ technique (either option): pinch a small fold of skin, insert needle at 45–90°, push the plunger slowly. ~60 seconds total per shot. The needle is shorter than most eyelashes — you'll barely feel it once you've done a few.

Tip 05 — Site-Targeted Injections Near the Knee (Optional, Accelerates Local Recovery)
The Safe Post-Op Injection Site Map

You have the option to inject BPC and Cartalax closer to the knee instead of in the abdomen. Same compound, same dose, same SubQ depth — just delivered into the local tissue around the surgical site instead of routed through the systemic circulation. Many post-op clients use this approach during the 8–12 week recovery window to accelerate local tissue healing on top of the systemic effect.

Why it works: BPC-157 and Cartalax both drive tissue-level healing signals. When you inject near a tissue that's actively remodeling (like your knee right now), you create a higher local concentration of the peptide at the exact site doing the most recovery work. The systemic effect still happens, but you stack a stronger local response on top.

The safe post-op injection site map — pick from these areas:

  • Upper thigh / vastus medialis area — ~5–7″ above the patella, on the inner thigh. Strong choice for routing BPC + Cartalax into the quadriceps-tendon side of the knee.
  • Upper calf / gastrocnemius area — ~3–4″ below the patella, on the upper calf muscle. Good for routing into the patellar tendon and below-knee tissue.
  • Outer side of thigh, just above the knee — lateral side, ~4″ above the patella. Hits the iliotibial band side.

What to AVOID — non-negotiable safety rules:

  • Stay ~2″ away from the incision line at all times. Do not inject through, over, or directly next to the surgical scar / sutures / healing wound.
  • Do not inject directly into the joint itself. Stay SubQ — just under the skin, not into muscle and definitely not into the joint capsule.
  • Do not inject into areas with visible redness, drainage, or unusual warmth — those are signs of localized inflammation or infection that need to be evaluated, not injected into.
  • If the site looks or feels wrong, default to the abdomen. The systemic SubQ route still delivers the peptide — you don't lose the effect, you just don't get the local amplification.

Technique (same as abdomen SubQ): pinch a small fold of skin, insert needle at 45–90°, push the plunger slowly. ~60 seconds per shot.
